# PROJECT PROPOSAL NEUROMATCH

Learning-related plasticity in visual cortex is usually attributed to reward and task feedback, but recent work (Zhong et al., 2025) shows most of it is unsupervised, driven by visual experience alone, while a distinct reward-prediction signal in anterior higher visual areas (HVAs) is the one clearly supervised component. We build on that dissociation by asking: which individual neurons encode reward, and how does the proportion of reward-encoding neurons — and how strongly they activate — change during learning?

Using the Zhong et al. two-photon mesoscope dataset (up to ~80,000 neurons across V1 and medial/lateral/anterior HVAs, supervised vs. unsupervised cohorts), we sample a large set of neurons from each region and classify reward-encoding neurons with the paper's own reward-prediction index: d′(late vs early cue), which compares a neuron's reward-zone activity between trials where the sound cue — whose position tracks the reward zone — arrives early vs. late. We cross-validate: a neuron counts only if |d′| ≥ 0.3 reproduces across two trial folds with the same sign, and we read chance against a label-shuffle null. We chose the cue-based d′ over an encoding-model reward ablation because the cue exists with or without water, which keeps the supervised-vs-unsupervised comparison fair — a water-based ablation would score the unrewarded cohort zero by construction — and matches the paper's Fig. 4f–g; we retain the ablation as a single-session validation. We then track both the proportion and the activation strength of reward-encoding neurons across training days, by brain region and cohort, treating each mouse as the replicate.

We predict that both the number and the activation of reward-encoding neurons rise with learning, concentrate in anterior HVAs, and largely vanish in the unsupervised cohort, mirroring the paper's reward-prediction findings.
